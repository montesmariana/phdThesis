8 The nature of clouds

Clouds come in many forms, as a result of a number of various characteristics

8.1 Introduction

Clouds come in many shapes. Like the cotton-like masses of droplets we see in our skies, the clusters of word occurrences generated by token-level distributional models may take different forms, depending on their density, their size and their distinctiveness. “Meaning is use,” “Differences in usage correlate with differences in meaning,” “You shall know a word by the company it keeps” and other such catchy slogans sound intuitively accurate, but they hide a wealth of complexity and variation. Like meaning, context is far from orderly, and a myriad of words with different characteristics interact to generate the variation we see in these clouds.

In this chapter, we will try to make sense of the nephological topology, i.e. the variety of shapes that these clouds may take. For this purpose, we will classify HDBSCAN clusters mapped to t-SNE representations in a way that can help us understand what we see when we see a cloud. The starting point is the shape that a researcher sees in the scatterplot, which will be visually mapped to meteorological cloud types and further described in technical terms.

In Section 8.2 we will discuss the rationale behind this particular classification and the tools used to operationalise these decisions. Section 8.3 will explore the technicalities that characterise the different types of clouds and their distribution across the lemmas. A more detailed description of each cloud type and their technical interpretations follows in Section 8.4, and finally we summarise the chapter in Section ??.

8.2 Rationale of the classification

In this document I will present the cloud classification, with cloud referring to an HDBSCAN cluster of a token-level vector space. As we will see below, we might have models with only one type of cloud, but typically they will have more than one. For each type, we will look at both a technical and a semantic interpretations, so that a future intrepid cloud spotter might know what to do or not to do with a given encounter. Types of clouds will be illustrated both with a picture of an actual cloud, taken from Wikimedia, and with plots from the vector spaces.

Understanding the complexity of the relationship between context words and clouds (or lack thereof) is crucial for the technical and semantic interpretations of all the clouds.

One thing to take into account is that the distances between tokens result from a complex interplay of features of their context words, but then both clustering and visualization add a layer of interpretation meant to find patterns of similar tokens that are different to the other tokens. Sometimes those patterns are easy to find, which leads to very nice, interpretable clusters (Cumulus, mostly); sometimes they are very hard to find, resulting in lots of noise and/or less defined clusters.

The factors that interact to produce a group of similar tokens include frequency, whether the context words co-occur or not, and their type-level similarity.

Clouds can be dominated by one context word that co-occurs with all the tokens in the cluster and does not occur outside the cluster. This perfect precision and recall of a context word for a cluster is not so common, but slightly perfect situations are quite common.

The cluster could instead be characterized by a group of context words that are similar at the type-level. They can be paradigmatic alternatives of each other or syntagmatic co-occurrences. The more similar two paradigmatic alternatives are at the type-level, the more they approximate one unique context word with their combined frequencies. In that sense, a cluster of 20 tokens co-occurring with the same context word may look the same as a cluster of 20 tokens co-occurring with 20 very similar context words.

Syntagmatic co-occurrences may enhance the similarity if their vectors are similar, or may be cause for splits when they are different. For example, 20 tokens co-occurring with the same context word, of which 10 occur with a very different context word and 10 don’t, will probably be split in two clusters. This is hard interpret from mere recall and precision values of a single cluster, since a key aspect is the complementarity between clusters, i.e. both clusters in this example would share a representative word and be distinguished by whether they also include the other representative word or not.

That covers the paradigmatic and syntagmatic relationship between context words and their corresponding similarity at type-level. However, whether they will actually create different clusters, either at HDBSCAN or, visually, on t-SNE, also depends on the frequency with which they co-occur and on the characteristics of the other context words. The relative frequencies and similarities plays a strong role in actually generating patterns. It’s very complicated and I don’t really know how to model it yet, but what I do know is that, whenever we interpret these clusters, technically or semantically, all these things play a role. Most often, a cluster dominated by one context word or dominated by three very similar context words are visually indistinguishable; whether two clusters have different main context words or they share one context word while they don’t share another is also visually indistinguishable.

I’ve divided the clouds in five categories plus a cross-cutting category. The classification is based on a combination of t-SNE visualization (perplexity 30) and HDBSCAN (minPts = 8) and it would probably be different if other visualization techniques or clustering algorithms are used.

The goal is to offer some guidelines on how (not) to interpret clouds, based on what they look like when we superimpose the HDBSCAN clustering to the t-SNE visualization (both based on the log-transformed cosine distances). A small number of steps based on formal criteria approximates the shape-based classification in a satisfactory manner.

The main categories, which I will describe in more detail below, are:

  • Cumulus: the most defined clouds;
  • Stratocumulus: a slightly looser definition of still decent clouds;
  • Cirrus: the weakest, smallest, less defined clouds;
  • Cumulonimbus: massive clouds;
  • Cirrostratus: the HDBSCAN noise (so, not a cluster).

The steps to classify the clouds rely on the following information:

  • whether the cluster is noise (since it is automatically named Cirrostratus);
  • the proportion of modelled tokens it covers;
  • the kNN (\(k=8\)) and SIL values (from semvar) using all the clusters, including noise, as classes on an euclidean distance matrix based on the t-SNE solution6;
  • the mean pairwise distance between tokens of that cluster mean distance;
  • the proportion of tokens in the cluster with an \(eps\) value lower than the minimum \(eps\) value for noise tokens, below_noise7.

In practice, the steps are as follows; each step works on the remaining clusters after applying the previous steps.

  1. Take all noise clusters, even in models with 100% noise. These are Cirrostratus.
  2. Take the clusters covering 50% of the tokens or more. These are Cumulonimbus.
  3. Take the clusters with kNN \(\ge\) 0.75, SIL \(\ge\) 0.5, mean distance \(\le\) 0.5 and either below_noise \(\ge\) 0.9 or in models with less than 10% noise. These are Cumulus.
  4. Take the clusters covering less than 10% of the modelled tokens, either in models with at least 75% noise or if kNN < 0.7. These are Cirrus.
  5. Take the clusters with kNN \(\ge\) 0.7, SIL \(\ge\) 0.5 or mean distance \(\le\) 0.2, as long as either below_noise > 0.5 or no more than 10% of the modelled tokens are noise. These are Stratocumulus.
  6. The remaining clusters are considered Cirrus.

In addition, the category of Hail groups the clusters with at least 8 identical tokens; these can belong to any of the other classes.

Cirrostratus and Cirrus are at the highest altitudes, which matches their tendency to have higher \(eps\) values. In absolute \(eps\) values, Cirrus clouds can be low, but (i) they are typically higher than Cumulus and Stratocumulus, (ii) their maximum value is typically higher as well and (iii) by definition they may have a substantial proportion of their points higher than the lowest Cirrostratus. The relative altitude between Cirrus and Cirrostratus is not perfectly mapped to \(eps\): here, the difference lies mostly in the small, disperse visual shape of the Cirrus clouds against the more uniform, large outline of Cirrostratus.

Cumulus and Stratocumulus are at the lowest altitudes, which maps to their tendencies to have lower minimum and maximum eps values. Again, the difference between them is defined in terms of shape: Cumulus are normally more compact and defined.

Finally, Cumulonimbus (formerly Mammatus) are, in the meteorological sense, massive and towering. This maps to both the visual and \(eps\) aspects: first, it covers a large proportion of the plot and visually dominates the model; second, it tends to cover a wide range of values. Figure ?? already shows that its maximum values are larger than for other types of clouds, except for Cirrostratus, while the minimum values are lower and more diverse. This is more clearly explained in terms of the range: the difference between the minimum and maximum \(eps\) within a cluster. Across all the clouds (not just in medoids), the minimum \(eps\) range in a Cumulonimbus clouds is 0.65 and the maximum is 4.48; 75% percent of these clouds have a range of 1.4 or more. In contrast, considering all the other clouds (even Cirrostratus), the \(eps\) ranges span between 0 (i.e. all points have the same \(eps\)) and 4.03, and 75% of these clouds have a range of 1.27 or less. In other words, these are massive, towering clouds.

There are more models with Cirrus clouds than with Cumulus clouds, but we can also see an inverse correlation between the proportion of models of a lemma that exhibit Cumulus clouds and the proportion that includes Cirrus clouds.

In the semantic interpretation, we will look at lemmas with particular tendencies, in terms of the proportion of models that have at least one cluster of a given type. I selected the most significant cases based on the \(\chi^2\) test: marked preference is understood as a residual above 2 or below -2.

  • Marked preference for Cumulus and against Cirrus: heffen, hachelijk, and schaal. Of these, heffen also has a marked preference for Stratocumulus.

  • Marked preference for Cumulus and against Cirrus, but also against Stratocumulus: gemeen, also the one with most Cumulonimbus clouds, and stof.

  • Marked preference for Stratocumulus and against Cirrus, but only slightly for Cumulus: heilzaam.

  • Marked preference for Cirrus and against both Cumulus and Stratocumulus: geestig, gekleurd and hoekig.

  • Marked preference for Cirrus and against Cumulus but only slightly for Stratocumulus: haten.

  • Highest preference for Cumulonimbus: gemeen again, but also stof and schaal (with residuals above 5; a few more have residuals above 2).

8.4 Types of clouds

8.4.1 Cumulus

The Cumulus clouds are those that look the best. They feel satisfying. In Figure 8.6, they are the green, blue, red and pink clouds of the plot; the rest are Stratocumulus.

Figure 8.6: Example of Cumulus cloud: inspiration on the left, plot example on the right. Picture Glg, edited by User:drini - photo taken by Glg, CC BY-SA 2.0 de, https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?curid=3443830.

They are defined by a number of different measures with strict values, after excluding Cirrostratus and Cumulonimbus. First, the clusters need to have both kNN \(\ge\) 0.75 and SIL \(\ge\) 0.5 for the t-SNE solutions, that is, based on the euclidean distances between tokens on the 2D mapping returned by t-SNE. The kNN value for a token indicates the proportion of \(k\) nearest neighbours (in this case with \(k = 8\)) that belongs to the same class, i.e. the same HDBSCAN cluster. The higher it is, the lower the overlap between clusters. However, it is biased towards large clusters, which are likely to have many tokens with a kNN of 1. Therefore, we also require a SIL (silhouette) value of 0.5 or higher, which is decently high for these results and hard to achieve in large clusters.

Next to the semvar measures, we also require the mean pairwise cosine distance between the tokens to be 0.5 or lower, to favour more compact clusters. The final requirement makes sure that the cloud stands out against the noise. One of the ways it can achieve this is by having an \(eps\) lower than the minimum noise \(eps\) in at least 90% of the tokens; however, in models without any noise or with very little, noise \(eps\) values might be particularly high (or, as it is calculated, the below_noise value is always equal to 0), so this threshold is not applied in models with less than 10% noise.

Most of these clouds are characterized by one context word with high precision and recall for the cluster. Specifically, the maximum F-score of a context word for these clusters is significantly higher than for any other type of cloud: 75% of these clusters have a context word with an F-score of 0.72 or higher, while in 75% of the rest of the clouds the context word with highest Fscore is not higher than 0.719. These top context words also tend to have high PPMI, but some may even have negative PMI.

The Cumulus clouds typically look very nice and tight and indicate very strong collocational patterns, but that does not mean that these are idiomatic expressions or that two Cumulus clouds in the same model are going to have different meanings. We’ll look at possible interpretations by inspecting some lemmas with the strongest preference for Cumulus clouds and against Cirrus: schaal, stof, hachelijk. gemeen and heffen also have a strong preference of Cumulus at the expenses of Cirrus. The former also rejects Stratocumulus and prefers Cumulonimbus, so it was already (briefly) described in section 8.4.4; the latter has a strong preference for Stratocumulus, so it will be discussed in section 8.4.2.

In the case of schaal, most of the Cumulus clouds are either represented by Richter/kracht (see examples (42) and (33)) or by gewicht, as shown in (43). These are examples of idiomatic expressions, where a fixed expression with a clear meaning beyond the componential combination of its elements can be identified. That is also the case of stof doen opwaaien and de grootste gemene deler (see (30) and (31)), next to other idiomatic expressions: they tend to be represented as Cumulus clouds. However, these idiomatic expressions do not necessarily include the target, e.g. in ere in (29). In addition, Cumulus clouds do not necessarily imply idiomatic expressions in this sense, as we can see from the rest of our examples.

Here we can see a combination of paradigmatic and syntagmatic relations. On the one hand, avontuur, onderneming (or, less frequently, zaak), moment, and positie are paradigmatic alternatives for the slot of noun modified by the target. They are also quite unequivocal indicators of one or the other sense, even if the difference between the senses is rather a matter of [temporal/telic] construal. At type level, situatie (which tends to occur in Stratocumulus) and positie are very similar, and onderneming is sometimes quite similar to situatie, but for the rest they are quite different from each other. In all cases, the patterns seem frequent enough to warrant distinct clouds.

At the same time, examples (55), (13) and (14) indicate typical examples from distinct clusters that persist across models and are the result of syntagmatic relations. The tokens co-occurring with positie — but not those co-occurring with other context words — may also co-occur with bevrijden (and uit), and additionally they might also include brandweer. The three patterns, characterized by the presence or absence of bevrijden and brandweer next to positie, are frequent enough (about 9 tokens each) to form distinct clusters. An important point is that, while brandweer and bevrijden are relatively close at type-level, they are very far from positie, which makes the tokens that co-occur with brandweer and/or bevrijden very different from those who don’t. And yet, the three small positie clusters are closer to each other in the t-SNE clouds than to the situatie cluster.

This is an important example of how clusters are the result of a combination of paradigmatic and syntagmatic relations between the context words. The cluster illustrated by (55) is not only characterized by the presence of positie but also by the absence of bevrijden and brandweer. In addition, the fact that this pattern occurs with positie but not with the very similar situatie, and in Belgian tokens but not in Dutch ones, indicates a local pattern that emerges in the context of hachelijk even if it’s not significant in the global scheme of the full corpus.

8.4.2 Stratocumulus

The Stratocumulus clouds are quite decent, although sometimes they look almost as good as Cumulus clouds, while in other cases they are much less clear. In Figure 8.7, all three clusters are Stratocumulus — indeed, they cover a rather wide range of respectable-looking clouds, with different sizes and degree of compactness.

Figure 8.7: Example of Stratocumulus cloud: inspiration on the left, plot example on the right. Picture by Joydeep - Own work, CC BY-SA 3.0, https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?curid=20357040.

The definition of Stratocumulus clouds takes a number of different measures and applies less strict thresholds than for Cumulus clouds. First the Cirrostratus, Cumulonimbus and Cumulus must classified, and the smallest cloudsm either in noisy models or without high kNNm must be reserved for Cirrus. On the remaining clusters we apply two filters. First, they must either have kNN \(\ge\) 0.7, SIL \(\ge\) 0.5 or mean pairwise cosine distance \(\le\) 0.2. In addition, either more than half the tokens have an \(eps\) value below the minimum noise \(eps\) value or the percentage of noise tokens in the model is lower than 10%.

Stratocumulus clouds are generally large: while 75% of either Cumulus or Cirrus have 28 tokens or fewer, half the Stratocumulus have 26 or more. However, in comparison to Cirrus they tend to have lower type/token ratio of context words9 and higher F-score values of their representative context words. In addition, the mean cosine distance between the tokens tend to be comparable to that in Cirrus clusters, in spite of the difference in size; in other words, they are larger but more compact and more clearly defined.

While lemmas that prefer Cumulus clouds tend to avoid Cirrus clouds and vice versa, the relationship with Stratocumulus is not so straightforward. A preference for Cumulus tends to go hand in hand with a preference for Stratocumulus, but there are some reverse cases and even some in which both Cirrus and Stratocumulus are preferred to the disadvantage of Cumulus (haten) or the other way around (gemeen and stof).

If we discard the hope to map clusters to senses, but decide to just interpret them in terms of collocational patterns, Stratocumulus clouds group the transition step between the very firm patterns of the Cumulus clusters and the weaker ones in Cirrus clusters. Given that the same context word can represent clusters of different types in different models (see examples below), the proportion with which they go for one or the other may give us an indication of the strength of the pattern.

To illustrate the semantic interpretation for Stratocumulus clouds, we will look at two lemmas with strong preference towards this type: heffen, which also has a strong preference for Cumulus and against Cirrus, and heilzaam, which also strongly rejects Cirrus and slightly prefers Cumulus.

The most typical representative words in clusters of heffen are belasting, tol and accijns for the ‘to levy’ sense and glas and hand for the ‘to lift’ sense, as illustrated in examples (46) through (19). Together, these context words represent 86.3% of all the Stratocumulus clouds of heffen and 92.7% of all its Cumulus clusters, but with different tendencies.

First, belasting mostly represents Stratocumulus clouds (79.9%), because of its frequency and its tendency to bring some tokens with similar contexts under its wing. In contrast, Cumulus is preferred by accijns (NA%), tol (69.7%) and glas (62.9%), which tend to have both perfect precision and very high recall for their respective clusters. The first two are just frequent enough to merit their own clusters, but glas occurs around 30 times in the sample (against 40-50 for belasting).

Finally, hand represents Cumulus and Stratocumulus clusters equally (46.6% and 47.7% respectively); its frequency is slightly lower than glas, but its recall is substantially lower for its clusters. In fact, this is another example of combination of paradigmatic and syntagmatic relationships next to frequency and distinctiveness. An algorithm that only captures the most relevant context word of a cluster will fail to find that the hand clusters also include tokens co-occurring with the paradigmatic alternatives arm and vinger (see examples (20) and (21)), whose type-level vectors are very similar. In addition, about half of the tokens in these clusters also co-occur with hemel, which is not a paradigmatic alternative of the body parts and, in a few models, may take the role of most representative context word. However, its effect is different from brandweer and bevrijden in the case of hachelijk because its type-level vector is close to that of the body parts. The internal consistency of these body-parts clusters, in terms of mean cosine distances, is lower than those of the other clusters — even the belasting clouds and even as a Cumulus — but it is clearly uniform enough to form one cluster.

The models of heffen are particularly nice in that they tend to have quite defined Cumulus or Stratocumulus clusters with identifiable representative context words that, at the same time, are very good cues for the predefined sentences. But this is by no means something to take for granted, as the models of heilzaam show us.

The two senses of heilzaam correspond to literal ‘beneficial for the health, healthy’ and a metaphorical/broader ‘beneficial’ sense. Unfortunately, its most frequent context words and representatives of their clusters are not distinctive of any sense but occur equally with both of them. An interesting point is that these were also tagged as cues by the annotators, for both senses. These context words, illustrated in ((heilzaam/werking?)) through (26), are the nouns werking, effect and invloed and the verbs werken and zijn. Together, they cover 89.7% of all the Stratocumulus clouds of heilzaam and 91.5% of all its Cumulus clusters.

The most frequent context word, werking, forms Stratocumulus clouds 63.5% of the time, because, while both kNN and the mean cosine distance are good enough for Cumulus clouds, the SIL values are rather low; notably, within the medoids it tends to form Cirrus clouds instead. In contrast, invloed prefers Cumulus 64% and effect represents both equally (40.3% and 42.1%). All three form Cirrus clouds 1 out of 10 times across all the clusters they form. At type-level, the three of them are usually very similar, especially invloed and effect, but clearly they are frequent enough to warrant separate clusters. Werking can also be seen close to werken. The verbs, on the other hand, are less consistent, with zijn preferring both Stratocumulus and Cirrostratus (as expected, it is not such a strong cue), and werken, Cumulus and Cirrus, to about 40% each.

Like the clusters of heffen, the clusters of heilzaam tend to be characterized by one powerful context word, with a high PPMI with the target type (except for zijn), that overrides the influence of other co-occurring context word and their mutual similarity. Nonetheless, unlike the representative context words of heffen, those of heilzaam are not good cues for the predefined senses.

It is an empirical question how often the contexts grouped in HDBSCAN clusters are homogeneous in terms of particular semantic phenomena, but heilzaam shows that it is not something to take for granted. What is more, it illustrates how neither a high PPMI nor their selection as cues by human annotators guarantee that a context word distinguishes predefined senses. When it comes to PPMI, it is understandable: the measure is meant to indicate how distinctive a context word is of the type as a whole, in comparison to other types. It does not take into account how distinctive it is of a group of occurrences against another group of occurrences of the same type. When it comes to cueness annotation, however, we could have expected a more reliable selection, but apparently the salience of these context words is to high for the annotators to notice that it is not distinctive of the different senses.

8.4.3 Cirrus

The Cirrus clouds are typically small, disperse clusters. In Figure 8.8, both clusters belong to this category. These are clusters in models with lots of noise and/or disperse clusters that don’t stand out without color coding.

Figure 8.8: Example of Cirrus cloud: inspiration on the left, plot example on the right. Picture by Dmitry Makeev - Own work, CC BY-SA 4.0, https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?curid=85153684.

They are defined as small clusters in noisy models or without kNN, next to the remainder of the clusters after defining Cirrostratus, Cumulonimbus, Cumulus and Stratocumulus.

Cirrus clouds are generally small, like Cumulus clouds: in a few cases they cover more than 100 points, but then the silhouette is too low and either the kNN or the percentage of tokens below noise is too low to be considered Stratocumulus. In any case, 75% of these clouds have 28 tokens or fewer. In spite of their size, they have a high high type/token ratio of context words and a low top Fscore, even compared to larger Stratocumulus: they have less of a tendency to be represented by single powerful context words. They also tend to have a higher entropy, regardless of the entropy of the model and in spite of their smaller size.

As we saw before, context words that represent Stratocumulus clouds can also represent Cirrus clouds — they are not necessarily bad, useless clusters. But, by tendency, they are defined by weaker patterns, and when they are the dominant type of cloud in a lemma, they suggest a a rather uniform internal structure. In fact, there is a moderate but significant inverse correlation between the proportion of Cirrus clusters for a lemma and the median Fscore of their most representative context word.

To illustrate the semantic interpretation of Cirrus clouds, we will look at some lemmas that prefer them while avoiding Cumulus: gekleurd, hoekig and geestig, which also avoid Stratocumulus, and haten, which does prefer Stratocumulus. In addition, both geestig and haten tend to have noisy models.

These four lemmas, next to hemels, are the ones with a larger proportion of noise tokens in their models; in geestig in particular, 10% of the models are only Cirrostratus. They are also among the six lemmas with the lower mean number of clusters per model (as low as 2.415 for geestig, and up to 3.175 for hoekig).

8.4.4 Cumulonimbus

The Cumulonimbus category (Figure 8.9) is the least frequent, but when it does occur, it dominates the plot. Almost all lemmas have at least one such medoid, and a few have two or up to four medoids dominated by Cumulonimbus clouds. Considering all models, gemeen, stof and schaal are the lemmas with a largest proportion of medoids with Cumulonimbus clouds, between a third and half the models.

Cumulonimbus clouds are minimally defined as clusters that cover at least 50% of the modelled tokens, including those discarded as noise. In practice, half of them cover at least 58.7% or more, reaching as much as 95.7%. Next to them, we typically have one more cluster (85.6% ignoring the noise); occasionally we may have two (11.1%) or even up to 5. They mostly co-occur with Cumulus clouds (0% of all these models have only Cumulus next to the Cumulonimbus; 54.8% have exactly one) but all combinations are attested.

Figure 8.9: Example of Cumulonimbus cloud: inspiration on the left, plot example on the right. Picture by fir0002flagstaffotos [at] gmail.comCanon 20D + Canon 17-40mm f/4 L, GFDL 1.2, https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?curid=887553.

This configuration results from a tight and/or small group of tokens that are very different from everything else. The “everything else” forms the Cumulonimbus cloud: large, normally disperse, and may seem to have inner structure captured by t-SNE but not by HDBSCAN. The small group, has a minimum of 8 tokens (the minPts argument for HDBSCAN) and mostly (75%) less than 30.

In these circumstances, we find that the small partner cloud is dominated by either one context word or a number of extremely similar context words that are very different from the rest. Some examples of dominant context words for the small partner cloud are werpen and richten in models of blik (examples (68) and (69)), ere in models of herstellen, as in (29), and the combination of doen and opwaaien in models of stof, as in (30).

Cases of groups of topically similar context words are football terms in models of haken (e.g. the 5 context words with highest precision in one such cluster are strafschop, strafschop_gebied, zet_om, penalty, doorgebroken), food in terms of hemels (e.g. gerecht, garnaal, groente, smaak, dressing), cars in terms of grijs (e.g. Mercedes, Opel, Ford, zie, steel), and proper names in models of hoopvol (e.g. aldus, Hecke, Rompaey, Coster, Winter).

The rest of the tokens, i.e. the Cumulonimbus cloud, has no uniforming structure, but instead is defined against this stronger, small cloud. Cumulonimbus clouds are not huge clusters of similar tokens, but a mass of tokens that is not structured enough in opposition to the distinctive small cloud that is next to it. It may have some form of inner structure: in fact, sometimes the small partner does not stand out as much as its inner dense areas in the t-SNE visualization. We might find relevant context words for them, especially if they are tight enough to be also considered Altocumulus clouds. But the reason they are a cluster is not because the tokens are similar to each other, as much as because the tokens in the small partner are similar to each other and different from everything else. The mean distance between tokens in a Cumulonimbus cloud is typically very large, sometimes as large as within Cirrostratus (noise), and significantly larger than within other kinds of clusters (although the few examples of Cirrus and Stratocumulus co-occurring with Cumulonimbus also have large mean distances).

The small partner cloud(s) in models with Cumulonimbus indicate either a very strong collocation (often an idiomatic expression!) or a strong topical tendency, depending on the number of context words involved (see note at the beginning). However, the large cluster groups all the rest, obscuring the inner structure. Sometimes, the visualization shows clear dense areas within the large cluster.

The small cloud is typically found in other medoids of the same lemma as well; it is very informative, as it shows a rather reliable collocation, but might also be too powerful. While the small groups make sense, the Cumulonimbus itself hides variation that we might be interested in.

If the Cumulonimbus cloud makes any sense at all, it is by coincidence. For example, two huldigen medoids have Cumulonimbus clouds, where the smaller partners (either as one Stratocumulus or as two Cumulus and one Stratocumulus) are characterized by the co-occurrences of opvatting, standpunt and principe, which are strong indicatives of the ‘to hold (an opinion)’ sense. This split matches our sense disambiguation, because all the remaining tokens, i.e. those of the ‘to pay homage’ sense of huldigen, are gathered in the Cumulonimbus cloud. However, that is just a side effect of the fact that the ‘to hold (an opinion)’ sense has a clear, coherent set of characteristic context words that set themselves apart from everything else, where “everything else” just happens to be a dictionary sense. In terms of type-level distances, for example, the mean distance between tokens of the same cluster is quite high for both Cumulonimbus and Stratocumulus clouds (much more than for Cumulus), and the maximum distance within the Cumulonimbus cloud is the highest, almost as much as the noise.

As we have mentioned before in Section ??, the lemmas with the strongest preference for Cumulonimbus clouds are gemeen, stof, and schaal, followed by spot. These lemmas tend to have some very strong context that separates a set of tokens from everything else regardless of the internal structure of the rest of the tokens or even the variety of context words co-occurring within the smaller cluster. These strong context words include deler for gemeen (example (31)) and opwaaien for stof (example (30)). In the case of schaal, in one of the medoids the context word is Richter, while in the other, it’s kracht, complemented by other earthquake-related context words (examples (42) and (33)); they both represent the same set of tokens, but Richter has the part-of-speech name and is therefore excluded from FOC-POS:lex models. In both cases, this smaller cloud covers the ‘range of values’ sense of scale, leaving the rest of the senses of either homonym in the same Cumulonimbus cluster. Crucially, the characteristic context words are not particularly representative of the sense, which could, in theory, include instances of de schaal van Celsius, for example. Instead, this sense is almost exclusively found in the context of earthquakes, in the expression de schaal van Richter. Finally, in spot models we also find two possible (sets) of context words representing the small cloud against Cumulonimbus clusters. On the one hand, we have drijven (example (34)), which is a good indicator of the ‘ridicule’ sense, is itself quite frequent and makes for large clouds contrasting the Cumulonimbus clusters. On the other, we have 1ste and two more almost identical context words that are excluded by FOC-POS:lex models and occur in a very particular context that seems to be a headline (see (35)).

In conclusion, Cumulonimbus clouds occur in lemmas where one or a few similar context words may group togethers very closely and far away from the rest, making the differences between the rest of the tokens pale in comparison to the difference with the smaller group. The small cloud is typically but not necessarily a Cumulus, and the large one that gathers the “rest” category is the Cumulonimbus, which does not have a grouping characteristic but is instead built in opposition to the more powerful one.

Lemmas that barely have any Cumulonimbus clouds, such as herroepen, hoekig, diskwalificeren and horde, for which at most 5% of the models have Cumulonimbus clouds, lack such a strong pattern and have, instead, groups with similar frequencies and mutual differences.

8.4.5 Cirrostratus

We will first, briefly, mention the Cirrostratus type (Figure 8.10), i.e. the noise according to HDBSCAN. It lies in the background of (almost) all our clouds and constitutes 100% of two of our medoids. Considering the entirety of our models, 146 (0%) of them are fully Cirrostratus clouds.

It might be interesting to cluster the subset of tokens that make up these clouds, at least for some lemmas, but that is not pursued in these studies. We will mostly ignore these clouds, but they are always present and affect how other clouds are defined.

8.4.6 Hail

Unlike the rest of the configurations (and breaking the metaphor of cloud shapes), Altocumulus clouds occur across all classifications. In Figure ??, both the clouds are Altocumulus: the small, orange one is a Cumulus and the large light blue one, a Cumulonimbus. These are clusters with at least 8 (\(minPts\)) identical tokens, defined as having a cosine distance lower than 10^{-6}.

For this reason, I might rename them with something that is not the shape of a cloud. To avoid “bubbles,” maybe “hail” or “snow.” In fact, in relative numbers the cloud type that occurs in this shape the most is Cumulonimbus, which is a storm cloud! Followed by Cumulus and Stratocumulus, which have higher absolute frequencies, but in relative frequencies less than 10% are “Altocumulus/Hail.”

5% of the clusters of about any type, in 924 different models, have these characteristics.

These conditions are prompted by a low number of context words per token and a low type/token ratio of these context words. These are groups of identical tokens, which isolates extremely strong collocations but erases all nuance, e.g. distances between groups and prototypicality of specific examples.

When these occur in Cumulonimbus configurations, we have a combination of very few context words per token (often only one) so that a small group with the exact same word(s) or, rarely, a few very similar ones (e.g. body parts in hoekig), stands out against all the rest… which itself might have inner bubbles.

This happens in very restrictive models where many tokens can be grouped together because they have identical vectors: they shared the few words that survived the thresholds.

These clusters often reveal the strongest context words, i.e. those that dominate in other clusters. They might match our expectations (like in heet, herroepen, hemels) or not (like in staal and spot). In the latter case, the problem is not too much noise but either not enough noise or wrong selection.

When there is not enough noise, the many identical tokens form tight and distinct clusters which we would rather group together, and tokens that don’t co-occur with the most powerful context words will be invisible. In that case, a slightly less restricted model might be more useful: in a way, noisy context words, often hopeless against the strong dominating context words, smooth the differences between the tokens to give us a more complex picture.

The wrong selection of context words means that context words that survive the restriction are not informative/useful, i.e. light verbs, determiners or prepositions. The same context words may become harmless noise in less restrictive models, but they might also keep their power if the less resilient context words are not strong enough. In that case, we might want to generate new models that exclude these particular context words, dependency relations, parts of speech, etc.

We might be tempted to consider these clusters idiomatic expressions: they match, visually, what we think a representation of idiomatic expressions would be like. Instead, they match groups of context words that occur very frequently in a very short distance (either in terms of bag-of-words or dependency relations) to the target. It tells us something about bigrams: about how often niet occurs close to harden; van to staal, op to spot, or hangijzer to heet. We still need to check which context words are responsible for the resulting structure and how they occur in the context.

8.5 Summary {theo2-summary}

Clouds come in many shapes. Like the cotton-like masses of droplets we see in our skies, the clusters of word occurrences generated by token-level distributional models may take different forms, depending on their density, their size and their distinctiveness. “Meaning is use,” “Differences in usage correlate with differences in meaning,” “You shall know a word by the company it keeps” and other such catchy slogans sound intuitively accurate, but they hide a wealth of complexity and variation. Like meaning, context is far from orderly, and a myriad of words with different characteristics interact to generate the variation we see in these clouds.

In this chapter, we will try to make sense of the nephological topology, i.e. the variety of shapes that these clouds may take. For this purpose, we will classify HDBSCAN clusters mapped to t-SNE representations in a way that can help us understand what we see when we see a cloud. The starting point is the shape that a researcher sees in the scatterplot, which will be visually mapped to meteorological cloud types and further described in technical terms.
